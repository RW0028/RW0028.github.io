About

I am an undergraduate student at the University of California, Davis, pursuing a double major in Neurobiology, Physiology & Behavior and Music. I am on a neuron-centered neuroscience track, with primary academic interests in understanding neural systems at the neuronal and circuit levels and how neural activity gives rise to physiological function.

As a pre-med student, I approach neuroscience not only as an intellectual pursuit, but also as a discipline closely connected to human health and clinical reality. This perspective motivates my focus on mechanisms that bridge neural activity and physiological function, while maintaining a long-term interest in translational relevance.

Alongside my core academic training in neuroscience, I maintain a sustained engagement with music as a parallel discipline. This background informs how I think about human experience and expression, but my academic and research trajectory is firmly grounded in neuroscience and neural systems.

Research

Current Research

  • Research assistant in a neuroscience laboratory at UC Davis, focusing on neural systems and physiology-related questions with an emphasis on neuronal-level mechanisms, including literature review and neural signal analysis.

Past Research Experience

  • Prior research experience in neurodegenerative disease mechanisms, with a focus on Alzheimer’s disease and the role of microglia in neural pathology. This work examined cellular stress responses, including endoplasmic reticulum stress, oxidative stress, autophagic–lysosomal pathways, and neuroinflammation, and resulted in a conference paper presentation.
    “Different Roles of Microglia in Alzheimer’s Disease,” International Conference on Biological Engineering and Medical Science (ICBioMed), 2023.
  • Past research experience in engineering- and computation-oriented projects, primarily involving dry-lab and data-driven analysis. This work contributed to my technical foundation and exposure to quantitative approaches, though my current academic interests have since shifted toward neuroscience and neuron-centered biological questions.
